The linux kernel development process

the linux kernel development process

Contents
  • Developers (that's you) make "patches" -- sets of changes -- to the source code
  • These patches are sent via email to the Linux Kernel Mailing List (linux-kernel@vger.kernel.org) or one of the subsystem mailing lists (e.g.: perhaps the linux-scsi mailing list linux-scsi@vger.kernel.org). Note: don't try to use Outlook, Yahoo mail, Gmail, Hotmail, as they will destroy your patches, and don't send patches as attachments, they should be inline. Use a mailer like pine or mutt which will preserve your patch intact.
  • The patch is reviewed, you fix whatever they complain about, (or argue why your way is better.)
  • The maintainer of whatever subsystem, and/or Andrew Morton incorporates your patch into their tree. (Andrew Morton's "MM" tree is here: http://userweb.kernel.org/~akpm/mmotm/, in patch form.)
  • Eventually your patch(es) move into Linus's tree.
There is kind of a schedule though:
  • After each release of the kernel (watch http://kernel.org), there is a "two week merge window" during which patches for the next kernel are submitted.
  • After the two week merge window closes, it's only minor bug fixes until the next kernel comes out and the next merge window starts.
  • It's generally best to have your patches already in some maintainer's sub tree prior to the merge window.
  • You don't have a lot of control over when or if your patches will make it into anyone's tree -- is this a bit frustrating? Yes, yes it is. That's just how it is.
